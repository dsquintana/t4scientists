Twitter threads

You can‚Äôt say a lot in a 280-character tweet, but you can with a Twitter thread, which connects a series of tweets. In principle, there is no limit to how many tweets can go in a thread; however, Twitter will only let you post 20 consecutive tweets in a single instance. I think this is a sensible limit for a thread, but you can always add additional tweets after you‚Äôve posted your first 20, if you like.

One benefit of using the Twitter app on your smartphone is that you can save your tweets as drafts‚Äîthis includes threads as well. So, this means that you don‚Äôt have to write a thread in a single sitting. Simply select ‚ÄúCancel‚Äù when writing a tweet, then select ‚ÄúSave draft‚Äù.

5.0.1 Using a twitter thread to announce a new paper

Threads are a great way to announce a new paper, as you can say much more than if you were posting a single tweet. I think threads are a much better way to announce your new papers than a blog post, because they‚Äôre quicker to write and are more likely to be shared. Unless you add an approximate reading time for your blog posts, people don‚Äôt know what to expect when they see a link to a blog post in Twitter. But threads are typically no longer than 20 tweets (and usually shorter), so people are more likely to start reading. In addition, you never know which tweet in your thread people will find particularly interesting. If you have a 20-tweet thread, then you have 20 tweets that can be potentially retweeted.

For more on why you should write threads, check out this thread.

Going viral

It‚Äôs almost impossible to predict which tweets will go viral, so don‚Äôt spend time trying to make a viral tweet. I made the following tweet on a whim, thinking a few psychological scientists who are familiar with the rabbit-duck illusion would find this funny. I was spectacularly wrong.

Rabbits love getting stroked on their nose pic.twitter.com/aYOZGAY6kP

— Dan Quintana (@dsquintana) August 18, 2019

While viral tweets have the upside of getting you a few more followers, Twitter will be essentially unusable for you during this period, as your notifications will be flooded. It‚Äôs typically tweets that cover general topics that go viral, so you probably won‚Äôt gain many new followers who are actually interested in your science‚Äîbut if you‚Äôre lucky you might get a few. I have found that the further your tweet travels outside of your network, the more unusual the responses. You may also get a few nasty tweets as well, which I cover in the next chapter.

Making your own GIFs

GIFs get a lot of attention. For instance, instead of just posting a static image of your paper, you can create a GIF that scrolls through your paper. Of course, you could post this as a video, but people often expect audio when they come across a video. GIFs don‚Äôt have audio and continually loop. There is a huge library of GIFs online and you can also search for GIFs directly in Twitter when composing a tweet.

If you would like to make GIFs on your desktop, I recommend the GIF Brewery app (Mac only), as this gives you lots of options for making GIFs. For your smartphone, I recommend ImgPlay (iOS and Android). The best part of this app is that you can squeeze the size of your GIFs to under the 5MB Twitter limit. You can‚Äôt do this with GIF Brewery app, and you‚Äôll have to sometimes play around with the settings to get your GIF under the 5MB limit.

Getting your tweet back in the feed again

No matter where you are in the world, some of your followers will be asleep when you tweet. This means that they might miss your tweet. Don‚Äôt think too much about ‚Äútiming‚Äù your tweets. However, if you‚Äôve tweeted something important, like a new paper, there are ways to ‚Äúre-introduce‚Äù your tweets to your user‚Äôs feeds.

1. Retweeting your original tweet. I would keep this to minimum, as this is the digital equivalent of patting yourself on the back, but this is fine occasionally.

2. Replying to your own tweet with a tweet containing additional information. By doing this, your followers will see your original tweet (despite the fact it was tweeted in the past) and the new tweet.

Sharing your replies

If you‚Äôve written a reply to someone‚Äôs tweet, the only time it will appear in someone else‚Äôs feed is if they follow both you and the person you replied to, or if the twitter algorithm somehow pushes this tweet into people‚Äôs feed if it‚Äôs produced a lot of likes or retweets. However, if you want more people to see it, you can retweet that particular reply tweet.

The Tweetdeck app

This web app, which can also be downloaded as a Mac app, is Twitter on steroids. You can customise this app with different columns, which can contain your main feed, the feed of a specific user (including yourself), notifications, a specific search term, the tweets you‚Äôve liked, and your messages.

Figure 5.2: Tweetdeck set up with four columns. Note that the fourth column is for keeping track of the PhDChat hashtag

Tweetdeck can be particularly useful if you have more than one twitter account (e.g., your individual account and an account for your lab) of if you‚Äôre following a specific hashtag or search term. You can create a column with any lists that you‚Äôve put together, which I covered in the previous chapter.

But I should warn you, this app can be very addictive because it can show you multiple streams of new information.

Highlighting your work on other platforms

Twitter‚Äôs popularity is not going to last forever (remember MySpace?). It‚Äôs hard to predict the next big social media platform, but it‚Äôs either going to be predominantly driven by text, video, or audio. If it‚Äôs text-based platform, then by getting involved with Twitter now, you‚Äôll get valuable experience. If you don‚Äôt want to get involved on Twitter because it‚Äôs a fad, you‚Äôll never get involved on any network‚Äîthey‚Äôre all fads in the long run. However, if the next big thing is audio or video, then you can use Twitter to highlight your posts on these types of networks.

5.0.2 Video

YouTube is a natural home for any videos you may have of presentations. It‚Äôs fairly straightforward to save a video of any online presentations that you give via Zoom or Microsoft Teams (just ask for people‚Äôs permission to record and share beforehand in case they appear in the video).

Even if you don‚Äôt use YouTube, this is the first point of call for millions of other people when they‚Äôre wanting to learn about a new topic.
